The SaaS Demand Generation Playbook: From MQL to Revenue

📅 February 5, 2025 ⏱ 10 min read

Most SaaS marketing teams are drowning in MQLs that never convert. Sales blames marketing for lead quality. Marketing blames sales for slow follow-up. Sound familiar? The problem isn't your team — it's your demand generation model. Here's how to fix it.

The MQL Model Is Broken (Here's Why)

The traditional MQL model worked when buyers followed a predictable journey: download whitepaper → attend webinar → request demo → buy. That linear path barely exists anymore.

Today's B2B buyers complete 70% of their research before ever talking to sales. They're reading your competitor's blog posts, watching YouTube comparisons, asking peers in Slack communities, and checking G2 reviews — all before you even know they exist.

When you gate everything behind a form and score it as an "MQL," you're measuring engagement with your content, not purchase intent. That whitepaper download? They might be a student writing a thesis. That webinar attendee? Could be a competitor doing research. The gap between content engagement and purchase intent is where most SaaS marketing budgets go to die.

Demand Creation vs. Demand Capture

Here's the framework that changes everything: separate your strategy into demand creation and demand capture.

Demand capture targets people who are already looking for a solution. These are high-intent buyers searching for terms like "best CRM for startups" or "Salesforce alternatives." Capture them with SEO, paid search, comparison pages, and review sites. This is your short-term revenue engine.

Demand creation targets people who have the problem but aren't actively searching for a solution yet. These are your future customers. Reach them with ungated educational content, podcasts, social media, community engagement, and thought leadership. This is your long-term growth engine.

Most SaaS companies over-invest in demand capture (because it's easier to measure) and under-invest in demand creation (because the ROI takes longer to show). The companies growing fastest in 2025 are doing both.

Building a Content Engine That Drives Pipeline

Content is the fuel of demand generation, but most SaaS content is forgettable. "5 Tips for Better Project Management" — who cares? Your competitors published the same article with 7 tips.

Content that drives pipeline has three characteristics: it's opinionated (takes a clear stance), it's specific (addresses a precise pain point for a precise audience), and it's actionable (the reader can implement something immediately).

Instead of "How to Improve Customer Retention," try "Why Your Churn Rate Doubled After Raising Prices (And the 3-Email Sequence That Fixes It)." See the difference? The second one speaks to a specific pain, implies expertise from experience, and promises a concrete takeaway.

Build your content calendar around the questions your best customers asked before they bought. Interview your sales team: what objections do they hear? What alternatives do prospects compare you to? What's the "aha moment" that makes people choose you? That's your content goldmine.

Paid Channels That Actually Work for SaaS

Google Ads remains the highest-intent paid channel for SaaS. People searching for your category terms are ready to buy. But CPCs are brutal — $15-50+ per click for competitive SaaS keywords.

The play is to own the long-tail. Instead of bidding on "CRM software," target "CRM for real estate teams under 50 people." Lower volume, lower CPC, higher conversion rate, better customer fit.

LinkedIn Ads are excellent for reaching specific job titles and companies, but they're expensive. Use them for retargeting warm audiences (website visitors, content engagers) rather than cold prospecting. A $50 CPM on cold traffic is wasteful; a $50 CPM on people who've read 3 of your blog posts is a bargain.

The emerging channel to watch is creator partnerships. B2B influencers on LinkedIn, YouTube, and newsletters can drive qualified pipeline at a fraction of the cost of traditional paid channels. The key is finding creators whose audience matches your ICP, not just those with the biggest following.

Measuring What Matters: Beyond MQLs

If MQLs aren't the right metric, what is? Here's the measurement framework we recommend for SaaS demand generation.

Leading indicators: website traffic from target accounts, content engagement depth (time on page, not just pageviews), demo requests from organic sources, and brand mention volume. These tell you if your demand creation is working before it shows up in pipeline.

Pipeline metrics: qualified pipeline generated (not just MQLs), pipeline velocity (how fast deals move through stages), win rate by source, and CAC payback period by channel. These tell you if your demand capture is efficient.

Revenue metrics: net new ARR by marketing source, expansion revenue influenced by marketing, and customer lifetime value by acquisition channel. These tell you if you're acquiring the right customers.

The companies that grow fastest are the ones that can clearly articulate how marketing activities connect to revenue, even if the connection isn't always direct or immediate.

▸ FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS

Q: How long does it take for demand generation to show results?

Demand capture activities (paid search, SEO for high-intent keywords) can show pipeline results within 30-90 days. Demand creation activities (content, brand building, community) typically take 6-12 months to show measurable pipeline impact. The best approach is running both simultaneously.

Q: What's a good CAC for SaaS companies?

CAC varies dramatically by ACV. A common benchmark is that CAC should be recoverable within 12-18 months. For SMB SaaS ($1K-10K ACV), target CAC of $500-3,000. For mid-market ($10K-100K ACV), $5,000-25,000 is typical. Enterprise ($100K+ ACV) can justify $50,000+ CAC.

Q: Should SaaS companies gate their content?

Our recommendation: gate nothing in the awareness stage, gate selectively in the consideration stage (deep research reports, tools/calculators), and make the demo/trial request the primary conversion point. Ungated content builds trust and reach; selective gating captures high-intent prospects.
